# Executive Summary

<p className="doc-meta">Exercise 1 · Documentation critique &amp; rewrite, delivered as an audit-backed strategy proposal</p>

**What I did.** I reviewed docs.mistral.ai the way a new developer arrives at it: one job, *build an AI application*, and no map of Mistral's product names. I walked that path end to end and marked every place it broke. The docs are technically rich and often excellent, so the problem I hit was structural, not editorial. The site is organised around Mistral's product surfaces and audience labels, not around what the reader is trying to do.

**What I found.** The content is hard to discover through the current IA: navigation routes by product name and audience label, so you need to know what Mistral calls something before you can find it. Search ends up doing the job the navigation should. My score is **findability 4/10**, scoped on purpose: findability is the axis I actually tested, and it gates the rest, because the best page is worthless if the reader can't reach it. (On content quality, which I did not audit site-wide, the one path I ran end to end reads as good content with thin edges. The concrete gaps are in [Exercise 3](/exercise-3-api-validation).)

## Where the developer path broke

Getting Started worked. Then three things stopped me, all about *where things live*, not how they are written:

- **The API-key gate.** The key lives in **Studio**, which presents itself as a standalone product, so I had to decode "Studio" to learn it was just the console I needed.
- **No Build front door.** Guidance on building (conversations, agents, RAG) is filed three ways at once: under Studio, under a separate **Developers** section, and as quickstarts in Getting Started. Nowhere single to aim at.
- **A product picker, not a path.** The platform overview opens *"Mistral AI has three products. Pick the one that matches what you want to do."* That is a menu of products where the reader has a job.

All three are structural. That points to one cause.

## The core finding

All three breakages share one cause: **the navigation names an audience where it should name work.** `Developers` is a top-level section, yet the developer's real workspace (Studio) sits inside `Products`, and what `Developers` mostly holds is reference that isn't developer-only, since an admin reads the changelog too. Audience labels can't keep up with a reader spectrum that now runs from infrastructure engineers to Vibe users who never touch the API; task labels don't have to. The same failure repeats at page level: look-up content has no single home, with the glossary in Get Started, the crawler spec at the site root, and the error glossary blended with 125 teaching cookbooks.

## What I propose

Replace the audience-named top navigation with a six-section, intent-based architecture, and move every page to the section that matches the reader's job.

| Today: audience &amp; surface named | Proposed: intent named |
|---|---|
| Getting Started · Models · Products · **Developers** · Admin · **API** | Get Started · Models · **Build** · Products · Admin · **Reference** |

The substantive change is dissolving `Developers`: its guidance goes to **Build**, its reference to **Reference**, and the developer persona becomes a landing-page entry point. Over the sections sits one axis the taxonomy leaves out, a **managed/self-hosted surface** (paired entry points, per-page tags, version-gating), because whether you run Mistral as SaaS or on your own hardware is a surface, not a section. Building finally gets a front door, and Studio drops back to the console it is.

The [Recommendations](/exercise-1/recommendations) turn this into a phased plan. The core re-cut ships as page moves and redirects with no rewrites, though the 301s have to be complete and permanent so search rankings and inbound links survive; the managed/self-hosted surface axis is a larger, separate content-engineering effort, sequenced after the quick win. The brief also asks for a rewrite, and my answer is that the highest-value change is structural, with the page-level craft shown in full on the [Example Rewrite](/exercise-1/example-rewrite). The docs also have a machine audience now, and the same canonical-URL discipline fixes it ([R4](/exercise-1/recommendations#r4)).
