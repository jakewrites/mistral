# Executive Summary

<p className="doc-meta">Exercise 1 · Documentation critique &amp; rewrite, delivered as an audit-backed strategy proposal</p>

I reviewed the Mistral docs the way a new developer actually arrives at them: with one job, *build an AI application*, and no map of Mistral's product names. This summary follows that journey, because where it broke is exactly where the information architecture does. The docs are technically rich and, in places, excellent. The problem I hit was structural, not editorial: the site is organised around Mistral's product surfaces and audience labels, not around what a developer is trying to do.

## The journey, and where it broke

[Getting Started](https://docs.mistral.ai/getting-started/platform-overview) worked: I found my use case and got oriented. Then it stalled in three places, and all three are about *where things live* rather than how they are written:

- **The API-key gate.** To make a call I needed a key, and the key lives in **Studio**. But Studio presents itself as a standalone *product* with its own getting-started, when all I actually needed was the console to get past step one, so I had to decode "Studio" before I could proceed.
- **No Build front door.** The docs on *building* (conversations, agents, RAG) sit filed under **Studio**, again under a separate **Developers** section, and again as quickstarts back in Getting Started. Three homes for "how do I build," and no single **Build** section to aim at.
- **A product picker, not a journey.** The [platform overview](https://docs.mistral.ai/getting-started/platform-overview) opens *"Mistral AI has three products. Pick the one that matches what you want to do."* That is a product menu where a developer needs a path: *build an AI application*.

This is the developer's walk, the one I could test end to end. It is one of three primary journeys, and the [Audit](/exercise-1/audit-findings#three-journeys) shows the same architecture serving the other two readers, the non-technical Vibe user and the admin, from one structure.

## The score, and where I aimed it

The brief is deliberately open, so the first judgement is where to aim. I aimed at information architecture because findability is the binding constraint on a platform expanding this fast: new and renamed surfaces (Le Chat became Vibe, La Plateforme became Studio, on-prem grew), a widening model lineup, and readers who now range from non-technical Vibe users to infrastructure engineers. Reorganising is also the cheapest and most time-sensitive fix: it raises findability without rewriting content, and structure only gets more expensive to change as the site grows.

The brief asks for a score. Mine is **findability: 4/10**, and it is deliberately scoped. Findability is the axis I actually assessed, and the one worth acting on. I did not audit content quality (I didn't test every page, verify code, or hunt for gaps), so I won't put a number on what I didn't measure. Findability gates everything: the best page is worthless if the reader can't reach it. This proposal moves that number without touching content.

## On the rewrite

The brief also asks for a rewrite. My conclusion is that the highest-value change is structural: a page-by-page rewrite treats the symptom, not the cause. The craft itself is demonstrated on the [Example Rewrite](/exercise-1/example-rewrite) page, where the page that broke the walk above, the live platform overview, is rewritten in full against the live copy.

## The core finding

The three breakages in the journey share one cause.

> The navigation names an audience where it should name work. `Developers` is a top-level section, yet the developer's actual workspace, Studio, sits inside `Products`, and what `Developers` mostly holds is reference (changelogs, SDKs, the error glossary) that is not exclusive to developers: an admin tracking new model releases reads the changelog too. The audience label fails in both directions, and each new persona or product multiplies the split. Mistral's audiences now run from infrastructure engineers to Vibe users who never touch the API and just write prompts. Audience-named sections cannot keep up with that spectrum, but task-named ones do not have to.

The same failure repeats at page level: look-up content has no single home. The glossary sits in Get Started, the crawler spec at the site root, and the error glossary in a Resources bucket blended with 125 teaching cookbooks. Canonical content, one home and one URL per thing, is the through-line of this proposal.

And once you are inside, the nav stops orienting you: `Products` holds both Studio and Vibe with no breadcrumbs or persistent "you are here", so a reader dropped in by search cannot tell which product they are in ([wayfinding](/exercise-1/audit-findings#wayfinding)).

## The proposal in one sentence

Replace the audience-named top navigation with a six-section, intent-based architecture, **Get Started · Models · Build · Products · Admin · Reference**, and move every page to the section that matches the reader's job.

| Today: audience & surface named | Proposed: intent named |
|---|---|
| Getting Started · Models · Products · **Developers** · Admin · **API** | Get Started · Models · **Build** · Products · Admin · **Reference** |

The substantive change is dissolving `Developers`: its guidance goes to **Build**, its reference to **Reference**, and the landing page becomes the developer's entry point. The **API** reference tab folds into **Reference**. On top of the sections sits one control the taxonomy deliberately leaves out: a **managed/self-hosted surface axis** (paired entry points inside Products, per-page surface tags, and version-gating for self-hosted). Whether you run Mistral as SaaS or on your own infrastructure is a *surface*, not a section. The reader's journey (orient, choose, build, run) decides where a page lives. The tab names follow from that.

In journey terms: building finally gets a **Build** front door, and Studio drops back to the console tool it is, documented in Products.

## Why this is low-risk

Execution is bounded: the Developers content re-homes into Get Started, Build, and Reference, four redundant pages gain redirects, and two look-up pages change parent. High navigational payoff, low engineering effort.

## Also built for the agent reader

Mistral's docs now have a machine audience: its own models, third-party coding assistants, and Vibe itself fetch and cite these pages, and an *"is it agent ready?"* probe is exactly the kind of check that circulates about an AI company. Right now that probe returns **7/100**: no `robots.txt` (it 404s), no content negotiation (the per-page Markdown `llms.txt` advertises 404s), and broken links inside `llms.txt` itself. These are config and build fixes. No new content is needed. It is the same canonical-URL discipline the human recommendations need, one audience further out. The evidence and the fixes are in [R3](/exercise-1/recommendations#r3).
